Tongue-tie, or ankyloglossia, is a condition that can significantly impact a baby’s ability to feed, affecting both breastfeeding and bottle-feeding. This often unrecognized or overlooked condition can pose challenges for new parents and may even affect speech development later in life. In this article, we will explore what it means to have a tongue-tied baby, the symptoms and diagnosis process, and the available treatment options.

This congenital condition causes the tissue connecting the tongue to the floor of the mouth (the lingual frenulum) to be shorter or tighter than usual. This restriction can make it difficult for a baby to latch effectively during breastfeeding, affecting their ability to feed and gain weight. Treatment for tongue-tie varies based on severity, with some cases requiring minor surgical intervention, while others may be managed with feeding support.

How to Identify Tongue-Tie in Babies

Identifying whether your baby has tongue-tie can be challenging, as symptoms vary in severity. Here are some common signs to watch for:

  • Difficulty latching: Babies with tongue-tie often struggle to achieve a deep latch, which is essential for efficient breastfeeding.
  • Prolonged feeding sessions: Because tongue-tied babies may not get enough milk in one feeding, they may feed more frequently or for longer durations.
  • Poor weight gain: Inadequate milk intake due to feeding challenges may result in slower weight gain.
  • Clicking sounds while feeding: If you hear clicking sounds while your baby is breastfeeding, this could indicate a poor latch due to restricted tongue movement.
  • Mother’s discomfort: Mothers may experience sore or cracked nipples, as babies with tongue-tie often clamp down during feeding to compensate for a weak latch.

Who Identifies a Tongue-Tie?

Tongue-tie is often identified during the newborn stage by a pediatrician, lactation consultant, or pediatric dentist. The diagnostic process includes a physical examination of the baby’s mouth to assess the length and mobility of the frenulum.

Treatment Options for Tongue-Tied Babies

Treatment varies depending on the degree of restriction and its impact on feeding. Here are the primary options:

  • Monitoring: For mild cases, healthcare providers may suggest monitoring the condition, as some babies adapt without intervention.
  • Lactation support: Working with a lactation consultant can help mothers improve latching and maximize milk intake. Sometimes, positioning adjustments can minimize the impact of mild tongue-tie.
  • Frenotomy: A minor procedure in which the frenulum is cut to release the restriction. This procedure is typically quick, performed in a doctor’s office, and usually causes minimal discomfort with a low risk of complications.
  • Frenuloplasty: In cases where the frenulum is thicker or requires more precise correction, a frenuloplasty may be recommended. This procedure requires anesthesia and sutures to achieve the best results.

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) supports frenotomy as a treatment option for tongue-tie babies experiencing feeding challenges. While the procedure carries minor risks, such as bleeding or infection, complications are rare. Most babies recover quickly and show immediate improvement in their ability to latch and feed effectively.

What Happens if the Tongue-Tie Is Not Addressed?

If left untreated, a tongue-tie can potentially affect a child’s ability to eat, speak, and perform oral hygiene tasks. Some children adapt well, while others may continue to experience issues. Speech delays, difficulty pronouncing certain sounds, and even dental issues can arise as a result of an untreated tongue-tie.
