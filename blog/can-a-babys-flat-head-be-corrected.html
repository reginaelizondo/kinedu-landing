“Flat Head Syndrome,” encompassing both plagiocephaly and brachycephaly, occurs when an infant’s head develops an uneven or misshapen appearance, often due to prolonged pressure on one side. This article will explore essential aspects of the condition, including its causes, different types, how to identify it, potential complications, and available treatment options.

This syndrome often occurs when babies lie on their backs or one side for extended periods, causing flattening on one side of the head (plagiocephaly) or across the entire back of the head (brachycephaly).

While flat head syndrome can raise concerns for parents, it’s important to note that most cases are primarily aesthetic rather than life-threatening. The condition can often be corrected through simple adjustments in positioning and increased tummy time. If these measures don’t work, early intervention can effectively address the issue.

How to Identify Flat Head Syndrome

Parents may notice a flat spot on the back or side of their baby’s head or asymmetry in their child’s facial features or ear alignment. Checking for these signs early and consulting a pediatrician can help confirm a diagnosis and determine if any intervention is needed.

Are Infant “Head-Shaping” Pillows Safe?

Many products, such as infant “head-shaping” pillows, claim to prevent or treat flat head syndrome. However, these are not recommended by health authorities like the American Academy of Pediatrics due to suffocation risks. Ensuring adequate tummy time and adjusting sleeping positions are safer and more effective practices.

How Can Flat Head Syndrome Be Treated?

Treatment varies based on severity. Mild cases often respond well to increased tummy time and changes in position. For more pronounced cases, a cranial helmet may be prescribed to gently mold the baby’s head over time. However, it’s essential to note that such interventions should always be recommended and monitored by a qualified specialist to ensure the best outcome for your baby.

When to Seek Help?

If parents notice consistent asymmetry in their child’s head shape or if the flatness doesn’t improve with repositioning and tummy time, it’s best to consult a pediatrician. In the meantime, engaging in activities to help can make a difference, such as frequently picking up your child, encouraging them to turn their head to the less preferred side, or playing games and positioning toys to motivate movement and exploration.
